04 How I see it

Agentic AI is one side of what I do. I decide which agents are worth building, and get them working in production.

Not every agent is worth building.

Agentic systems are unpredictable. They improvise, they drift, and most of them die before they reach production. Getting one to survive takes three things: the judgment to pick the few worth building, the discipline to make them reliable, and the work to get agents and people working together. That matters most in regulated and sovereign settings, where someone has to answer for the result.

Three reasons an agent dies before production

No owner

When it goes wrong, no one is responsible. Without that, people never trust it enough to rely on it.

No eval

It worked in the demo, but no one measured it in real use. So when it starts to drift, nobody notices until it causes a problem.

No know-how

It was plugged in but never taught how the work actually happens: the steps, the exceptions, the rules nobody writes down. So it answers confidently and gets the real cases wrong.

All three are gaps between business and tech. Closing them while the project is live is easy. The test is whether the agent still works six months later, once the team that built it has moved on.
