02 How I see it

Most agents shouldn't be built.

Agentic systems behave more like people than software: they improvise, they drift, and most of them die before they ever reach production. So the work that matters isn't building more of them. It's the judgment to know which few are worth it, the discipline to make them reliable, and the orchestration of agents and humans to deliver the outcome, especially in regulated and sovereign environments, where accountability isn't optional.

Three reasons an agent dies before production

No owner

When it goes wrong, no human is on the hook. So it never earns trust, and trust is what gets it deployed.

No eval

Working in the demo isn't a reliability bar. With nothing measured, drift stays invisible until it's expensive.

No know-how

The agent got wired up but never given the real functional knowledge of how the work is actually done: the process, the exceptions, the unwritten rules. It sounds fluent and gets the real cases wrong.

03 What I do

Plenty of people can write the strategy or stand up the agent. What's scarce is the call on which agents are worth building at all, and the standing to make that call land on both sides of the table: the executive's and the engineer's. That's where I work.
